# Tax Planning Strategies

## Tax-Advantaged Account Limits (2025)

| Account | Limit | Catch-up (50+) | Total (50+) |
|---------|-------|-----------------|-------------|
| 401(k) / 403(b) | $23,500 | $7,500 | $31,000 |
| Traditional IRA | $7,000 | $1,000 | $8,000 |
| Roth IRA | $7,000 | $1,000 | $8,000 |
| HSA (Individual) | $4,300 | $1,000 | $5,300 |
| HSA (Family) | $8,550 | $1,000 | $9,550 |
| 529 Plan | No federal limit | N/A | Gift tax above $18,000/yr |
| SEP IRA | 25% of comp, max $70,000 | N/A | $70,000 |
| Solo 401(k) | $23,500 employee + 25% employer | $7,500 | $70,000 total |

### Roth IRA Income Limits (2025)
- Single: Phase-out $150,000 - $165,000 MAGI
- Married filing jointly: Phase-out $236,000 - $246,000 MAGI
- Backdoor Roth: Available regardless of income (contribute to Traditional IRA, convert to Roth)

## Standard Deduction vs Itemized (2025)

| Filing Status | Standard Deduction |
|---------------|-------------------|
| Single | $15,000 |
| Married Filing Jointly | $30,000 |
| Head of Household | $22,500 |
| Additional (age 65+, single) | +$1,600 |
| Additional (age 65+, married) | +$1,300 |

### When to Itemize
Itemize when total deductions exceed the standard deduction. Common itemizable expenses:
- State and local taxes (SALT): capped at $10,000
- Mortgage interest: on up to $750,000 acquisition debt
- Charitable contributions: up to 60% of AGI for cash
- Medical expenses: exceeding 7.5% of AGI
- Casualty losses: in federally declared disaster areas

### Bunching Strategy
If total itemized deductions are close to the standard deduction, consider "bunching" deductions in alternating years:
- Make 2 years of charitable contributions in 1 year
- Prepay property taxes or state taxes (subject to SALT cap)
- Accelerate medical procedures into one calendar year

## Deductible Spending Categories to Track

Use Monarch Money categories and transaction searches to identify:

### Charitable Contributions
- Search terms: "charitable", "donation", "church", "tithe", "nonprofit", "red cross", "united way"
- Track both cash and non-cash (clothing donations, etc.)
- Donations > $250 require written acknowledgment
- Non-cash > $500 requires Form 8283

### Medical and Dental
- Search terms: "medical", "dental", "doctor", "hospital", "pharmacy", "prescription", "vision", "therapy"
- Only deductible above 7.5% of AGI threshold
- Include premiums paid with after-tax dollars, co-pays, prescriptions, dental, vision

### Education
- Search terms: "tuition", "education", "course", "training", "textbook"
- Educator expense deduction: $300 above-the-line (K-12 teachers)
- Student loan interest: up to $2,500 above-the-line (income limits apply)
- Lifetime Learning Credit: 20% of first $10,000 (income limits)
- American Opportunity Credit: up to $2,500 per student (first 4 years)

### Home Office (Self-Employed Only)
- Search terms: "office supplies", "internet", "desk", "computer"
- Simplified method: $5/sq ft, max 300 sq ft = $1,500
- Regular method: actual expenses x business-use percentage

### Business Expenses (Self-Employed)
- Search terms: "business", "client", "conference", "travel"
- Deductible on Schedule C
- Includes: supplies, software, professional development, travel, meals (50%)

## Tax-Loss Harvesting

### Rules
- Sell investments at a loss to offset capital gains
- Short-term losses first offset short-term gains (taxed as ordinary income)
- Long-term losses first offset long-term gains (taxed at 0/15/20%)
- Net losses can offset up to $3,000 of ordinary income per year
- Excess losses carry forward indefinitely

### Wash Sale Rule
- Cannot repurchase "substantially identical" securities within 30 days before or after the sale
- Applies across all accounts (including spouse's accounts and IRAs)
- Workaround: buy a similar but not identical fund (e.g., switch from one S&P 500 fund to another total market fund)

### When to Harvest
- Holdings with unrealized losses > $1,000 (to justify the effort)
- Especially valuable when you also have realized gains to offset
- End of year (October-December) is the most common window
- Can be done year-round if opportunities arise

### Tax-Loss Harvesting Candidates to Flag
Look at investment holdings and identify:
- Any holding with current value significantly below cost basis
- Pair with gains harvested in the same tax year
- Consider whether the holding still fits the portfolio strategy

## Year-End Tax Action Items

### October
- Review year-to-date income and estimate annual total
- Identify tax-loss harvesting candidates in taxable accounts
- Check 401(k) contributions — enough paychecks remaining to max out?

### November
- Execute tax-loss harvesting trades (allow settlement before December 31)
- Make charitable contributions (consider donor-advised fund for bunching)
- Review HSA contributions — max out if possible (triple tax benefit)
- Consider Roth conversion if in a low-income year

### December
- Final 401(k) contribution adjustment
- Last day for charitable contributions (December 31)
- Required Minimum Distributions if age 73+ (deadline December 31)
- Last day for tax-loss harvesting (trade must settle by Dec 31; allow T+1)
- Prepay January mortgage for extra interest deduction (if itemizing)

### January-April (Next Year)
- IRA contributions for prior tax year (deadline: April 15)
- HSA contributions for prior tax year (deadline: April 15)
- Gather tax documents (W-2, 1099s arrive January-February)
- File taxes or extension by April 15

## Key Tax Principles for the Advisor

1. **Never provide specific tax advice** — always recommend consulting a CPA/tax professional
2. **Identify opportunities**, not obligations — "you may want to discuss X with your tax advisor"
3. **Flag time-sensitive items** — contribution deadlines, year-end harvesting windows
4. **Track deductible spending** — Monarch Money categories make this easy
5. **Highlight under-utilized accounts** — especially HSA (often overlooked triple tax benefit)
